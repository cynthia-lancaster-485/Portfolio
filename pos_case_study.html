The problem

When staff don't trust their tools, the whole business slows down.

The existing POS had been in use for years, but the interface hadn't kept up. It looked dated, the visual hierarchy was unclear, and nothing felt reliable. Staff were hesitant always second guessing whether they'd pressed the right button, whether a transaction had gone through, whether the screen was frozen or just slow.

That hesitation has a real cost: slower checkouts, more errors, more stress on staff, and frustrated customers. The fix wasn't just cosmetic, it was about rebuilding confidence in the tool itself.

😬

Staff hesitation at every step

The unclear visual design made staff pause and second guess before every action, especially under pressure with customers waiting.

🕰️

Outdated visual language

The interface looked like legacy software. Staff didn't feel like they were using a professional, reliable tool and that perception matters.

❓

No clear feedback or hierarchy

Actions didn't confirm they'd worked. Important controls weren't visually distinct from secondary ones. Everything felt equally weighted.

The process

From observations to a 87-screen deliverable.

Without a formal UX team, I drove the process myself from understanding the problem to handing off a complete hi-fi prototype that developers could build directly from.

👁️
Step 01
Observe & Listen

Watched staff use the existing system in real transactions. Noted where they hesitated, what they clicked twice, and what made them sigh.

📝
Step 02
Define the gaps

Mapped the full transaction flow and identified every point where the design was working against the user instead of with them.

✏️
Step 03
Lo-fi → Hi-fi

Started with wireframe sketches to nail the layout and flow before investing in visual design. Iterated in Adobe XD through to a polished 87 screen hi-fi.

🚀
Step 04
Handoff & build

Delivered the complete prototype to the development team. The designs were built into a working POS system used by real staff every day.

Design decisions

What I changed and why.

Visual trust

Clean, modern visual language

Replaced the legacy aesthetic with a clean, high contrast design system. Clear typography, consistent spacing, and a reliable color language so staff always know what's interactive and what's informational.

Feedback

Every action confirms itself

Added clear confirmation states for every significant action button presses, completed transactions, applied discounts. Staff never have to wonder "did that work?" again.

Hierarchy

Primary actions are unmissable

Redesigned the visual weight of every screen so the most important action charge, confirm, next is always the most visually prominent element. Secondary actions step back.

Speed

Fewer taps for common tasks

Analyzed which workflows were most frequent and streamlined them. The most common transaction paths were reduced to the minimum number of steps without sacrificing accuracy.

Error prevention

Hard to make mistakes

Destructive or irreversible actions (voids, refunds, discounts) are visually distinct and require confirmation. The design makes the right thing easy and the wrong thing harder.

Scale

87 screens, fully consistent

Built a complete design system for the prototype reusable components, consistent patterns, and a logic that scales across every flow, from checkout to inventory to end of day reporting.

What I learned

Three things this project taught me.

01

Trust is a design outcome

Users don't articulate "I don't trust this interface" they just hesitate. Watching where staff paused taught me that confidence in a tool is something you design for explicitly, not accidentally.

02

Handoff is part of the design

Working directly with developers taught me that a great prototype is only half the job. Clear naming, component consistency, and annotated logic made the build go smoothly and stay true to the design.

03

Scale forces discipline

87 screens will expose every inconsistency in your design system immediately. Building with reusable components from the start wasn't optional it was the only way to keep 87 screens coherent.
